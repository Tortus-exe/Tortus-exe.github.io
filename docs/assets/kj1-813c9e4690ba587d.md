# Japanese Sentences

"\[Particles\] are the linchpins of the \[Japanese\] language". 

Japanese sentences always have the same structure. This structure is best visualized as a train with two parts: the main car, labeled <span style="color:red;">A</span>, and the engine, labeled <span style="color:#2575f6;">B</span>.

![image](?kawajapa_train)

There are always two kinds of sentences in Japanese:
1. <span style="color:red;">A</span> **does** <span style="color:#2575f6;">B</span>
2. <span style="color:red;">A</span> **is** <span style="color:#2575f6;">B</span>

We can change A or B as we please: describe A further, turn B into past tense, turn the sentence negative or into a question. No matter what we do, every Japanese sentence boils down to one of these kinds of sentences at a fundamental level. So how does this work in Japanese?

If we want to say something like <span style="color:red;">Sakura</span> <span style="color:#2575f6">walks</span>, this is a type-1 sentence: <span style="color:red;">A</span> **does** <span style="color:#2575f6;">B</span>. In this case, A would be さくら and B would be あるく (to walk). In order to turn this into a valid Japanese sentence, we need to add the *linchpin* to connect the two cars together. This linchpin is <span style="color:red;">が</span>.

![image_2](?kawajapa_sakura_ga_aruku)

So, the valid Japanese sentence is <span style="color:red;">さくら<strong>が</strong></span><span style="color:#2575f6">あるく</span>.

Let's look at the other kind of Japanese sentence: the <span style="color:red;">A</span> **is** <span style="color:#2575f6">B</span> sentence. Suppose we want to say Sakura is a Japanese person. <span style="color:red;">A</span> would be <span style="color:red;">さくら</span>, and <span style="color:#2575f6">B</span> would be <span style="color:#2575f6">日本人(にほんじん)</span>. As before, we would add <span style="color:red;">が</span>, but to make this kind of sentence, we must add *one more thing*: だ[^1]. You can think of だ as a one-way equals sign: Sakura is a Japanese person, but a Japanese person is not necessarily Sakura.

![image_3](?kawajapa_sakura_ga_nihonjin_da)

So in Japanese, we would say <span style="color:red;">さくら<strong>が</strong></span><span style="color:#2575f6">日本人</span>だ.

Note that in Japanese, adjectives that end with い carry a meaning of *being*. That is, あかい means *to be* red, and あおい means *to be* blue. Thus, we should not add another だ to the end of those sentences, because two equal signs is one too many.

# The Japanese Zero Pronoun

Some readers may notice that there are sentences that seem to be missing one of these carriages. To explain this, let's think about the English word "it".

In English, if I say "It is cloudy", "It" is the weather. If I say "It is mine", "It" could be referring to anything - my cup, my hat, my Evangelion merchandise. The point is that the English word "It" refers to something in context. Imagine if English did not require saying "it" all the time: "Is cloudy today." "My car was fine, suddenly made noises, started wobbling, lost a wheel." This is essentially the way Japanese works: there is a pronoun ø (pronounced "zero") that stands in for something in context. 

![image_4](?kawajapa_sakura_da)

For example, take the sentence "<span style="color:#2575f6">さくら</span>だ". This sentence seems to be missing the red main carriage. The full sentence is actually "<span style="color:red;">øが</span><span style="color:#2575f6">さくら</span>だ". In this case, ø stands in for 私(わたし), so the sentence means "I am Sakura". It's not that the main carriage is missing, it's simply invisible. Keep this in mind as sentences become more and more complex - the main carriage is **never missing, only invisible**.

Let us introduce another carriage to our train: the <span style="color:#35f152">を</span> carriage. This carriage stands in for the <span style="color:#35f152">object</span> of the sentence, which we can commonly think of as "the noun we are doing something to". Consider the following example:

![image_5](?kawajapa_keeki)

Notice that there are two core carriages, the red and blue. They are marked with exclamation points. This sentence is perfectly clear: I eat cake. It would also be valid for us to say the following:

![image_6](?kawajapa_keeki_zero)

Notice that this time, we have no 私が, and instead it has been omitted (as represented by my crude gray outline of the red train). The sentence here is read <span style="color:#35f152">ケーキを</span><span style="color:#2575f6">たべる</span>, which means "(ø) eats cake". So what does ø mean? Remember it means something in context, and the most natural thing to assume by default is the speaker: "I eat cake". However, if I were mentioning you, then ø would possibly refer to *you*: "You eat cake". Or, in a forest, seeing a rabbit come up to a slice of delicious chocolate mousse, ø might be "the rabbit": "The rabbit eats cake".
