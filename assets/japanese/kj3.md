# Japanese Verbs

Japanese Verbs always end in an う sound and fall into two classes:
1. 一段 (いちだん): verbs that end with iる/eる (たべる, ほめる, etc.)
2. 五段 (ごだん): all other verbs

We will now cover verb morphing rules for て and た form. Generally, て form is known as the connective form and is used to connect multiple ideas together, e.g. "I eat and drink." = "<span style="color:red;">(øが)</span><span style="color:#2575f6">たべて</span>、<span style="color:#2575f6">のむ</span>". た form is typically known as the past tense form, and is used whenever you wish to put something in past tense. e.g. "I ate." = "<span style="color:red;">(øが)</span><span style="color:#2575f6">のんだ</span>".

Category one has the easiest morphing rules: always drop the る and replace it with whatever you need. For example:
- たべる→たべて
- ほめる→ほめたい
- いる→いた

The second category has more complicated rules. We will break the second category into smaller groups:
1. verbs that end with う, つ, or る, called the うつる group
2. verbs that end with ぬ, ぶ, or む, called the new boom group
3. verbs that end with く, or ぐ, called the く group
4. verbs that end with す, called the す group.
5. する (to do)
6. くる (to come)

### 1. the うつる group
For the うつる group, て form and た form are made by removing the last kana and replacing it with って・った.
- よる→よって, to depend on
- まつ→まって, to wait
- つかう→つかって, to use

### 2. the new boom group
For this group, the て and た form are made by removing the last kana and replacing it with んで・んだ.
- よむ→よんだ
- しぬ→しんで
- よぶ→よんだ

### 3. the く group
For this group, replace く with いて・いた and ぐ with いで・いだ. You can remember this as one because if there is a dakuten on the く, then there is a matching dakuten on the て・た.
- きく→きいて
- そそぐ→そそいだ

**The only exception to this is いく, which when put in the て form is not いいて but いって, and in the た form is いった rather than いいた.**

### 4. the す group
For this group, す becomes して and した.
- うつす→うつして
- さす→さした

### 5. する
Treat する like す. It will become して and した.

### 6. くる
Treat くる becomes きて and きた.

# The Japanese Non-past Tense

The default form of Japanese verbs as you would find them in a dictionary is called the non-past tense. This means that this tense could mean something in the present, but typically defaults to something in the future. It operates much like the default form of English verbs: Next week, I fly to Tokyo. "来週(らいしゅう)、東京(とうきょう)にいく"

You could see this being used in a literary description for the present tense: "The sun sinks over the orange sea". However, this is rare in actual conversation. Some examples:
- 犬(いぬ)が食(た)べる: The dog will-eat
- さくらがあるく: Sakura will-walk

Suppose we want to say something more natural with the present tense. The Japanese form mirrors the english form - we use the verb "to be" (いる) as an auxiliary verb and the connective て form:
- 犬が食べている: The dog is-eating.
- さくらがあるいている: Sakura is-walking.

Notice that with this form, たべる would be an engine, but it just so happens that it is not - いる is the engine of this sentence. So, we will colour the engine white, to signify that it isn't actually doing the hard work of driving the sentence.

# The Japanese Past Tense

The Japanese past tense is as simple as can be: put the verb in た form, and it is in past tense. For example,
- 犬が食べた: The dog ate.
- さくらがあるいた: Sakura walked.

# Absolute time expressions

Sometimes you want to be more specific about what time something occurred. Say something happened on Tuesday, or in May. These expressions differ from expressions with relative time, such as "four hours later" or "three minutes ago".

A good rule of thumb Kawajapa brings up is to use the に particle to mark absolute time, and not for relative time. For example:
- 火曜日(かようび)に(øが)ケーキをたべる: I eat cake on tuesday.
- 六時に(øが)いった: I went at 6 o'clock.

But it's difficult to think of when is a relative time and when is an absolute time. Instead, Kawajapa suggests using に whenever English requires an "in", "at", or "on":
- <span style="color:red;">on</span> Monday
- <span style="color:red;">at</span> 6 o'clock
- <span style="color:red;">in</span> July

[^1]: You would say (respectively): "犬があるいていた" and "さくらが食べていた".
