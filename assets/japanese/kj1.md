# Japanese Sentences

"\[Particles\] are the linchpins of the \[Japanese\] language". 

<div>Japanese sentences always consist of two parts: <span style="color:red;">a thing/person (主語、しゅご)</span> and <span style="color:#2575f6;">what the thing does/is (述語、じゅつご)</span>. For example, the english sentence:</div>

<div><span style="color:red;">There is</span><span style="color:#2575f6"> a rabbit</span></div>

In Japanese, this sentence would be:

<div><span style="color:red;">ウサギ</span><strong>が</strong><span style="color:#2575f6">ある</span></div>

Where ウサギ means rabbit, and ある means *to exist*. As you can see, が is the particle that follows the *doer*. Some more examples:

1. <span style="color:red;">犬(いぬ)</span>**が**<span style="color:#2575f6">はしる</span>: dog is running
2. <span style="color:red;">はな</span>**が**<span style="color:#2575f6">あかい</span>: flower is red
3. <span style="color:red;">とり</span>**が**<span style="color:#2575f6">とんでいる</span>: bird is flying

Note that in numbers 1 and 3, the dog and bird are *doing* something. In number 2, the flower is *being* something. Both sentences use the が particle to mark the doer of the sentence. In fact, there must be a が in **EVERY** valid Japanese sentence.

Astute readers may notice that some Japanese sentences have no apparent が. For example, the sentence <span style="color:#2575f6">疲(つか)れた</span>. Japanese requires that every sentence **must** have a が somewhere - in this case, the full meaning of this sentence is <span style="color:red;">私</span>**が**<span style="color:#2575f6">疲れた</span>, where 私 (me) is the doer of this sentence.

Another example is the sentence <span style="color:#fcd135;">私</span>は<span style="color:#2575f6;">日本人</span>です. This means "I am a Japanese Person." This seems to break our rule yet again - the doer of the sentence is present but there is no が! The key to understanding this is the *Japanese zero-pronoun*.

# The Japanese Zero Pronoun

The zero pronoun, or øが (read "zero-ga") is a mysterious pronoun in that it is invisible when in a sentence. To see how this could make sense, let us take the english equivalent: "Lift the barbell, then place it on the rack." In this sentence, what is "it"? 

Of course, "it" refers to the barbell. Why can't we say "Lift the barbell, then place the barbell on the rack."? In English, this is a perfectly valid sentence, but it is *not a natural* sentence. If the listener already knows about the barbell, we would waste our energy by mentioning it again. 

Just like how the meaning of "it" depends on context, so too does øが. In the previous case, ø represents the aforementioned 私, the doer of the sentence. In another sentence, such as "<span style="color:#35f152">ミルク</span>を<span style="color:#2575f6">持(も)っている</span>？", meaning "Got milk?"[^1], we see the zero pronoun in action... in both languages! In English, who is the doer of the sentence "Got milk?" Obviously, it's "you"! (**Have you** got milk?). Similarly, the ø in Japanese is you. (<span style="color:red;">**あなた**</span>**が**<span style="color:#35f152;">ミルク</span>を<span style="color:#2575f6;">持っている</span>？).

The zero pronoun is not limited to *doers* of sentences either. Take the Japanese sentence "<span style="color:red;">私</span>が<span style="color:#2575f6">食(た)べた</span>". Literally, it means I ate. However, it is unclear what exactly I ate - another case for the ø pronoun. <span style="color:red;">私</span>が<span style="color:#35f152">ø</span>を<span style="color:#2575f6">食(た)べた</span>. This means "I ate 'it'". "It" could be anything... cake, some rice, whatever. But if I have previously mentioned some food, for example that I was craving tofu, then it likely means that I ate the tofu.

I wish to mention here and now that particles follow their nouns. **From now on, both nouns and particles will be appropriately coloured.**

# The Wa particle

Kawajapa opens with a joke regarding conventional Japanese learners. They often believe "<span style="color:#fcd135;">私は</span><span style="color:#2575f6;">ウナギです</span>" is some kind of colloquial contraction which means "I will have eel" in the context of a restaurant. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of the behaviour of <span style="color:#fcd135">は</span>. In particular, <span style="color:#fcd135">は</span> NEVER marks the doer of the sentence. In order to highlight the meaning of <span style="color:#fcd135">Xは</span>, we will translate it as "As for X" in English.

Let us look again at the sentence "<span style="color:#fcd135">私は</span><span style="color:#2575f6">日本人です</span>", which we now know to actually be "<span style="color:#fcd135">私は</span><span style="color:red;">øが</span><span style="color:#2575f6">日本人です</span>". When we translate, we get "**As for me**, ø is Japanese". Note that the doer of the sentence is marked with the <span style="color:red;">が</span> particle, and thus the <span style="color:#fcd135">は</span> particle cannot subvert that responsibility. <span style="color:#fcd135">は</span> has its own responsibility: to mark the topic of the sentence. It only just so happens that in this sentence, the topic and doer are the same thing.

But what about a sentence where this isn't the case? We look back at our original sentence, "<span style="color:#fcd135;">私は</span>(<span style="color:red;">øが</span>)<span style="color:#2575f6;">ウナギです</span>". We know this to mean "<span style="color:#fcd135">As for me</span>, <span style="color:red;">ø</span> is <span style="color:#2575f6">an eel</span>". So what's ø? From context, we infer that it isn't actually me - it's my order, the thing I want to eat.

Of course, if this context is not present, we cannot infer anything. By default, we assume the only thing that we can: that ø is the only other thing in the sentence - me! So if you ran up to me in the street and said "<span style="color:#fcd135;">私は</span>(<span style="color:red;">øが</span>)<span style="color:#2575f6;">ウナギです</span>", you really would be saying, "As for me, **I** am an eel". And you would be considered crazy.

We will really nail home the point of the wa particle by showing two sibling sentences:
- <span style="color:#fcd135">東京(とうきょう)は</span><span style="color:red;">øが</span><span style="color:#f05394">土曜日(どようび)に</span><span style="color:#2575f6">行(い)きます</span>
- <span style="color:#fcd135">土曜日は</span><span style="color:red;">øが</span><span style="color:#f05394">東京に</span><span style="color:#2575f6">行きます</span>

In the first sentence, we translate it as "As for Tokyo, ø (I) am going there on Saturday". In the second, we translate it as "As for Saturday, I will be going to Tokyo". We can see that the emphasis, the **focus** of each sentence is different, but the actual action is the same - I go to Tokyo on Saturday. *Wa does not explain what is going on, only what is the focus of a sentence.*

------------

[^1]: Of course, in English, "Got milk?" is not proper grammar... but in Japanese, ミルクを持っている？ is completely correct and valid formal grammar.
