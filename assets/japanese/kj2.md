# The は flag

Last article, we touched upon the core elements of Japanese sentences: the main car and the engine. We also introduced our first optional car - the を car. So, where does は fit into this picture?

It turns out that は can't be a core car - it can't be the engine or the main car because those roles are already taken. It can't be an optional car either. Those cars affect the logical structure of the sentence, which は does not do. So, what is は?

We will represent は as a flag, flying atop a car. It marks that car as the topic of a sentence. Take, for example, the sentence "<span style="color:#fcd135;">私は</span><span style="color:#2575f6">アメリカ人(じん)</span>だ". The sentence looks like this:

![image](?watashi_ha_amerikajin)

We notice that in the sentence, there is no が, so we imply one since it is a core car. Thus, the full Japanese form of the sentence is actually "<span style="color:#fcd135;">私は</span><span style="color:red;">øが</span><span style="color:#2575f6">アメリカ人</span>だ". This literally translates to "As for me, I am American". 

Failing to understand this can lead to some pretty hilarious misunderstandings. Suppose we are in a restaurant, and I tell the waiter "<span style="color:#fcd135">私は</span><span style="color:#2575f6">ウナギ</span>だ". If one were taught that "<span style="color:#fcd135">私は</span><span style="color:#2575f6">アメリカ人</span>だ" means "I am an American", then logically "<span style="color:#fcd135">私は</span><span style="color:#2575f6">ウナギ</span>だ" would mean "I am an Eel". Using what we just learned, however, we know the true meaning is "**As for me**, ø is an eel". What is ø? In the context of a restaurant, a waiter would easily interpret ø to be "my order" so that the sentence means "As for me, **my order** will be eel", or in other words, "I would like eel please".

# The に car

Now, we add another optional car: the に car. This car marks the target of what we are doing. To illustrate this, take the example sentence "<span style="color:red;">私が</span><span style="#35f152">ボールを</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投(な)げる</span>".

![image_2](?booru_nageru)

We see the function of the を car here: it describes the engine. The core sentence simply says "I throw" (<span style="color:red;">私が</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投げる</span>). Adding the を car specifies *what* I am throwing: the ball (<span style="color:red;">私が</span><span style="#35f152">ボールを</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投(な)げる</span>).

We can add even more information regarding the engine: <span style="color:red;">私が</span><span style="color:#35f152">ボールを</span><span style="color:#fc529f">さくらに</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投(な)げる</span>.

![image_3](?booru_sakura)

Notice the additional <span style="color:#fc529f">に</span> car. This tells us that the target of the throwing is Sakura, so in other words, "I throw the ball <span style="color:#fc529f">at/to Sakura</span>". 

We can shuffle the matchings of the words and particles to create completely new grammatical meaning. Try to use what you learned to understand what the following sentences mean.

- <span style="color:#fc529f;">私に</span><span style="color:red;">さくらが</span><span style="color:#35f152">ボールを</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投げる</span>[^answer-1]
- <span style="color:red;">ボールが</span><span style="color:#fc529f">私に</span><span style="color:#35f152">さくらを</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投げる</span>[^answer-2]

Thus, we can say whatever we want in Japanese, so long as we get the particles correct.

# は and the other particles

Let's look at our example and see what happens when we put は on the が train.

![image_4](?booru_ha_ga)

This says <span style="color:#fcd135;">私は</span><span style="color:red;">øが</span><span style="color:#35f152">ボールを</span><span style="color:#fc529f">さくらに</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投げる</span>. We know this to mean "As for me, I throw the ball at Sakura." What if we move the は to a different car, like the を car?

![image_5](?booru_ha_wo)

This says <span style="color:#fcd135;">ボールは</span><span style="color:red;">私が</span><span style="color:#35f152">øを</span><span style="color:#fc529f">さくらに</span><span style="color:#2575f6">投げる</span>, which means "As for the ball, I throw it at Sakura." Note that when we moved the flag, what is happening hasn't changed. Instead, we merely shifted the *focus* from me to the ball.

[^answer-1]: This sentence means "Sakura throws the ball to/at me".
[^answer-2]: This sentence means "The ball throws Sakura to/at me". Even though this sentence doesn't make much sense in real life, it might still be true in FantasyLand!
